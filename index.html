THE SOCCER BRIDGE

How star athletes and winning teams are created. A look at NYC high school soccer from the public and private sectors.

By Erica Soto & Luis Miguel Echegaray | @silentkillerica @lmechegaray | March 31, 2015

When it comes to finding athletic success at the High School soccer level in New York City there are many factors to consider: private training, natural talent, school funding, team diversity and head coaching to name a few. MLK Jr. High School is the most successful public school soccer team and operates with minimal resources. Dalton High School, a private school, has several resources at their disposal, but to date has only produced one winning season. Despite their lack of resources MLK Jr. is beating the odds and has produced professional and collegiate athletes. The Dalton School, which has a strong focus on academics, is still on a path to finding more athletic achievements.

Martin Luther King Jr. High School (Public)

Martin Luther King Jr. High School is a public school on the upper west side neighborhood of Manhattan, New York City, near Lincoln Center. Six small schools share the campus but they play together on a single soccer team.


MLK Jr. High School does not currently have a practice field and must rotate locations. The school has an indoor gym which has limited availability due to the high demand by other teams. Practice is usually located in Riverside Park but this all depends on how early the team can get there as there always competition from other schools or organizations that also need the space. Randall's Island is just not a possibility as the school doesn't have a bus service for after school activity and the trip to the island can take as long as an hour from the school's location. The team needs to walk or take the subway whenever they have practice. On game days, which are usually during the weekends, Randall's Island is available. These fields are usually coordinated by both schools who are playing with the help of the Public School Athletic League (PSAL), the organization who runs the public school athletic teams around the city. The program is led by head coach: Martin Jacobson.

The Dalton School (Private)

The Dalton School is a prestigious, highly selective private school on the upper east side of Manhattan, New York. The school has three buildings, they are all located on the Upper East Side: The first school, the main middle school and high school, and the athletic department.

The Dalton School is a member of the Ivy Preparatory School League in athletics. The Athletic department is located in their state of the art athletic complex, considered as one of the best in Manhattan. The Physical Education center is a 32,000 square-foot facility which houses two gymnasiums, a wrestling and aerobics room; a weight and fitness center; coaches office; a sports medicine office; locker facilities and classrooms. The outdoor sports teams utilize the soccer and baseball fields, track, and tennis courts at Randall's Island. Aside from the Physical Education complex, the team practices at Randall's Island Park by the East River in Manhattan. The outdoor space is a gigantic facility that offers soccer fields (grass and turf) to schools and organizations around the city. The team travels by bus transportation from the athletic department to the fields. It is a 25-minute commute without traffic. The athletic Department is responsible for field permits and transportation. Despite the fact that Dalton doesn't have a soccer field of it's own, they call Randall's Island their home ground. The Physical Education complex offers gym space (2) whenever Randall's Island is not available. The complex also allows for weight room space, cardio work etc. The school provides all uniforms and all necessary equipment. The school also hires a full time athletic training staff. The players have access to injury assistance and general medical attention. The program has 4-5 coaches, all led by head coach: Luis M Echegaray.

According to the Social Science Research Network diversity is good for soccer teams. Players may accrue additional benefits when their players differ in the way they interpret problems and use their skills to solve them. The results of the study, which includes the top five European soccer leagues, shows a positive relationship between diversity and performance that is visible even among the very best teams in the world. In the breakdown of diversity between MLK Jr. and Dalton you will both teams have varied players. However, Black and Hispanic players dominate the roster for MLK Jr. while Caucasian players are the team majority at Dalton.


PLAYERS BASED ON NYC BOROUGH

MLK Jr. Players have a much longer commute time than athletes at The Dalton School. Several commute from outer boroughs and create an additional sacrifice for the student athletes on this team. The team must also commute to practices around the city.

The majority of athletes at The Dalton School commute from Manhattan, while less than 1% commute from outer boroughs. The average commute for soccer players at The Dalton School is 15 minutes by train. The team attends practices at the school.

TEAM CHAMPIONSHIPS

Martin Luther King High School's boys soccer teams has dominated H.S. soccer for the better part of two decades and one reason the team comes from various neighborhoods is because Coach Jacobson recruits throughout New York City. The Dalton School is a highly selective, prestigious, private, coeducational college preparatory school and does not currently recruit athletes.

MLK Jr. is a powerhouse team in the PSAL league and is one of the top teams in the nation. The team has produced 14 city titles in the past 17 years. In 2014 the team ended their three-year run as city champs in a 1-0 loss to the 12th seeded school, Aviation.

The Dalton School has some of the finest indoor athletic complexes in Manhattan. The coaches and staff are strongly committed to the health and development of every individual student. In 2009 the team won its first, and only, NYSIAS championship.

POST GRADUATION SUCCESS

Less than three in 50, or about 5.7 percent, of high school senior boys interscholastic soccer players will go on to play men's soccer at a NCAA member institution. Only 1.9% go on to play professionally. However, MLK Jr. has produced 10 professional soccer players. Including New York Cosmos 21-year old attacker, David Diosa.
